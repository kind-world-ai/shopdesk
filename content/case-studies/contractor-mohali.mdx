## The Problem: ₹2.8 Lakh Sitting with a Client — And He Didn't Know

Harpreet Singh runs **HS Construction** from Sector 88, Mohali. Civil contractor. Roads, drainage, small commercial buildings. Mostly GMADA and private developer projects across Mohali and Zirakpur.

Harpreet is good at building. He's not good at paperwork.

### How Retention Works (And How It Gets Lost)

Every RA Bill in a construction contract has **retention money** — typically 10% of the gross bill amount, held back by the client as security. This money is supposed to be released after the **Defect Liability Period** (DLP) — usually 12–24 months after project completion.

Here's the problem: the contractor has to **claim** this money. The client won't send you a reminder saying "Hey, your retention is due." You have to track it yourself, calculate the exact amount across all RA Bills, and formally request release.

### Harpreet's Tracking System: His Head

Harpreet tracked retention the way most small contractors in Punjab do — **mentally**. He knew roughly how much was held back on each project, but "roughly" isn't a number you can put in a demand letter.

In early 2025, Harpreet had completed 2 projects where the DLP had already passed:

1. **GMADA drainage project, Sector 77** — completed June 2023, DLP ended June 2024
2. **Private builder compound wall, Zirakpur** — completed September 2023, DLP ended September 2024

Both projects had retention money sitting with the clients. Harpreet knew this "in general" but didn't have the exact figures, didn't know which RA Bills the retention came from, and hadn't sent a formal demand.

### The Bigger Problem: No Bill-Level Tracking

Harpreet's RA Bills were prepared by his billing clerk in Excel — a fresh file each time. No cumulative tracking. No running total of retention held. No payment aging. Each bill existed in isolation.

When Harpreet tried to calculate how much retention was outstanding from the GMADA project, he had to:
1. Find all the RA Bill Excel files for that project (scattered across folders)
2. Open each one individually
3. Find the retention line item in each payment summary
4. Add them up manually

He gave up after 45 minutes and 3 incomplete files.

## The Recovery: 3 Weeks, 2 Meetings, Full Payment

### Week 1: Formal Demand Letters

Armed with the exact bill-wise breakdown from ShopDesk, Harpreet's billing clerk prepared formal retention release demand letters for both clients. Each letter included:

- Project name and contract number
- Bill-wise retention breakdown (from the Payment Tracker)
- DLP completion date
- Total retention amount due
- Request for release within 15 days

### Week 2: GMADA Meeting

Harpreet met the GMADA engineer with the breakdown. The engineer verified the numbers against his records — they matched exactly. **₹1,23,000 was approved for release** the same week.

### Week 3: Zirakpur Builder Meeting

The private builder initially pushed back: "Let me check my records." Harpreet handed him the printed bill-wise statement from ShopDesk. The builder's accountant verified within 2 days. **₹1,57,000 was transferred** via NEFT the following week.

**Total recovered: ₹2,80,000.**

Money that was always rightfully Harpreet's — he just didn't have the system to track it and claim it.
