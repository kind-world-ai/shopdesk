## Quick Answer

An **RA Bill (Running Account Bill)** is a progressive payment invoice raised by a contractor to the client at regular intervals based on work completed to date. In India, RA Bills follow a standard payment waterfall: **Gross Amount → +GST 12% → Minus Retention 10% → Minus TDS 2% (Section 194C) → Minus Advance Recovery → Net Payable.** Most construction contracts — CPWD, PWD, private — use RA Bills for monthly or milestone-based payments.

## 1. What is an RA Bill?

A **Running Account Bill (RA Bill)** is a progressive payment invoice used in the Indian construction industry. Instead of waiting until an entire project is finished — which could take months or years — contractors raise intermediate bills at regular intervals based on the work completed so far.

### Why Contractors Use RA Bills

Construction projects are capital-intensive. A contractor building a ₹1 crore project cannot afford to wait 12 months for payment. RA Bills solve this by allowing contractors to receive payments as work progresses — typically every month.

This creates a healthy cash flow cycle:

- **Contractor** completes a month's work
- **Quantity surveyor** measures work done on-site
- **RA Bill** is prepared and submitted to the client
- **Client** verifies, deducts applicable amounts, and pays the net amount
- **Cycle repeats** until project completion

### Industries That Use RA Bills

RA Bills are standard in:

- **Civil construction** — buildings, houses, commercial complexes
- **Infrastructure** — roads, bridges, flyovers, metros
- **Government projects** — CPWD, PWD, NHAI contracts
- **Interior fit-outs** — large commercial interiors
- **Industrial construction** — factories, warehouses, plants

### Legal Basis in Indian Construction Contracts

RA Bills are referenced in all major Indian contract formats:

- **CPWD General Conditions of Contract** — Clause 7 covers running account payments
- **Indian Contract Act, 1872** — provides the legal framework for progressive payments
- **FIDIC Conditions** — used in international and large private contracts
- **Item rate contracts** — the most common format where RA Bills are used
- **PWD contracts** — state-level public works follow similar RA Bill procedures

> **RA Bill Definition:** A Running Account Bill is a periodic payment certificate issued in construction contracts, calculated based on cumulative work done to date minus amounts already paid in previous bills, with applicable deductions for GST, TDS, retention money, and advance recovery.

## 2. RA Bill vs Invoice: What's the Difference?

Many new contractors confuse RA Bills with regular invoices. They serve fundamentally different purposes.

| Aspect | Regular Invoice | RA Bill |
|--------|----------------|---------|
| **When raised** | After complete delivery | During ongoing work |
| **Frequency** | Once | Monthly or milestone-based |
| **Amount** | Full contract value | Partial (based on work done) |
| **Quantities** | Fixed, final | Cumulative — increases each bill |
| **Reconciled** | Final bill settles | Each RA Bill is progressive |
| **Deductions** | Standard GST/TDS | GST + TDS + Retention + Advance |
| **Used in** | Goods supply, services | Construction contracts |
| **Certification** | Not required | Needs engineer's measurement |
| **Legal standing** | Standard commercial | Contract-specific |

**Key insight:** An RA Bill is *cumulative*. RA Bill #3 shows total work done from day one — not just work done in month three. The current bill amount is the difference between the cumulative total and what was billed in RA Bill #2.

## 3. RA Bill Format in India: What Fields Are Required?

Every RA Bill in India must contain these fields to be accepted by the client:

### Header Information

- **Bill Number** — Sequential numbering: RA-01, RA-02, RA-03...
- **Contract Reference** — Contract number, work order number
- **Project Name** — Full name of the project
- **Contract Value** — Total agreed contract amount
- **Contractor Name** — With GSTIN and PAN
- **Client Name** — Owner, developer, or government department
- **Period of Work** — From date → to date for this bill

### Work Details (Linked to BOQ)

- **BOQ Item Number** — Reference to the Bill of Quantities
- **Description of Work** — Exact item description from BOQ
- **Unit** — Sqm, cum, rmt, kg, nos, etc.
- **Rate** — Agreed rate per unit (from contract)
- **Previous Total Quantity** — Cumulative quantity billed in all previous RA Bills
- **Current Bill Quantity** — Work done in this billing period
- **Total to Date** — Previous + Current
- **Previous Amount** — Rate × Previous Quantity
- **Current Amount** — Rate × Current Quantity
- **Total Amount to Date** — Rate × Total Quantity to Date

### Payment Waterfall Section

- Gross Amount (current bill)
- GST calculation
- Retention deduction
- TDS deduction
- Advance recovery
- Net payable amount

### Signature Fields

- Contractor's authorised signatory
- Client's engineer / quantity surveyor certification
- Date of submission and certification

### Sample RA Bill Format (3 Items)

Here's how a simplified RA Bill looks with three BOQ items:

| BOQ # | Description | Unit | Rate (₹) | Prev Qty | Curr Qty | Total Qty | Prev Amt (₹) | Curr Amt (₹) | Total Amt (₹) |
|-------|------------|------|-----------|----------|----------|-----------|---------------|---------------|----------------|
| 1.1 | Earth excavation | Cum | 250 | 400 | 120 | 520 | 1,00,000 | 30,000 | 1,30,000 |
| 2.1 | RCC M25 | Cum | 8,500 | 80 | 30 | 110 | 6,80,000 | 2,55,000 | 9,35,000 |
| 3.1 | Steel reinforcement | MT | 65,000 | 12 | 5 | 17 | 7,80,000 | 3,25,000 | 11,05,000 |
| | **Total** | | | | | | **15,60,000** | **6,10,000** | **21,70,000** |

In this example, the current bill gross amount is **₹6,10,000** — which then goes through the payment waterfall.

## 4. How to Prepare an RA Bill Step by Step

Follow these steps every billing cycle:

### Step 1: Gather Your BOQ (Bill of Quantities)

Start with the agreed BOQ from your contract. This lists every work item with descriptions, units, rates, and total quantities. Your RA Bill is built on top of this BOQ.

### Step 2: Record Work Done in the Current Period

On the last day of the billing period (usually month-end), the site engineer or quantity surveyor physically measures all completed work. This is called the **Measurement Book (MB) entry**.

Measurements must be:
- Verified on-site
- Recorded in the measurement book
- Signed by the site engineer

### Step 3: Calculate Current Bill Quantities

For each BOQ item:

**Current Bill Quantity = Total Measured to Date − Previous Bill Total**

This gives you the incremental quantity for this billing period only.

### Step 4: Multiply by Agreed Rates

For each item, calculate:

**Current Bill Amount = Current Bill Quantity × Agreed Rate**

Sum all items to get the **Gross Work Amount** for this RA Bill.

### Step 5: Apply the Payment Waterfall

This is where most contractors make mistakes. Follow the exact order described in Section 5 below. The waterfall includes GST, advance recovery, retention, and TDS — each applied to specific base amounts.

### Step 6: Get Client Engineer's Certification

The client's engineer or PMC must verify and certify the quantities. Without this certification, the client is not obligated to pay. Always get this in writing — not just verbal approval.

### Step 7: Submit to Client Billing Department

Submit the complete RA Bill with:
- Measurement book abstract
- Running account statement
- GST invoice
- Any supporting photographs or test reports

> **Pro tip:** Submit your RA Bill within the first 3 working days of the new month. Delays in submission = delays in payment. Many contractors in Chandigarh and Mohali lose 15–30 days of cash flow simply because they submit bills late.

## 5. The RA Bill Payment Waterfall: Indian Standard

### How Net Payable is Calculated in an Indian RA Bill

This is the most critical calculation every Indian contractor must understand. The payment waterfall determines how much money actually reaches your bank account.

**The standard sequence:**

### 1. Gross Work Amount

Sum of all work items for the current bill period.

**Formula:** Σ (Current Bill Quantity × Rate) for all BOQ items

### 2. (+) GST @ 12%

Most construction services fall under **HSN Code 9954** and attract **12% GST** (6% CGST + 6% SGST for intra-state, or 12% IGST for inter-state).

**Formula:** GST Amount = Gross Work Amount × 0.12

> **Note:** Some works contracts attract 18% GST. Government contracts for affordable housing may have 1% or 5% GST. Always check your specific contract and HSN code.

### 3. (=) Total Including GST

**Formula:** Total = Gross Work Amount + GST Amount

### 4. (−) Mobilization Advance Recovery

If the client gave you a mobilization advance at the start of the project (typically 10–20% of contract value), a proportional amount is recovered from each RA Bill.

**Formula:** Advance Recovery = Gross Work Amount × Recovery Rate (usually 20%)

Recovery continues until the entire advance is repaid.

### 5. (−) Retention Money

The client holds back a percentage as security for the **Defect Liability Period (DLP)**.

**Formula:** Retention = Gross Work Amount × Retention Rate (usually 5–10%)

Retention is typically capped at 5% of total contract value. Once the cap is reached, no further retention is deducted.

### 6. (−) TDS @ 2%

Under **Section 194C** of the Income Tax Act, the client must deduct TDS on payments to contractors.

**Formula:** TDS = Gross Work Amount × 0.02

> **Important:** TDS is calculated on the **gross amount excluding GST**, per CBDT Circular No. 23/2017.

### 7. (=) Net Payable Amount

**Formula:** Net Payable = (Gross + GST) − Advance Recovery − Retention − TDS

### Worked Example

Let's calculate an actual RA Bill for a ₹1 crore project:

**Given:**
- Contract Value: ₹1,00,00,000
- Gross Work This Bill: ₹8,00,000
- GST Rate: 12%
- Advance Recovery Rate: 20%
- Retention Rate: 10%
- TDS Rate: 2%

**Calculation:**

| Step | Description | Amount (₹) |
|------|------------|------------|
| A | Gross Work Amount | 8,00,000 |
| B | (+) GST @ 12% (A × 0.12) | 96,000 |
| C | (=) Total Including GST (A + B) | 8,96,000 |
| D | (−) Advance Recovery (A × 20%) | 1,60,000 |
| E | (−) Retention Money (A × 10%) | 80,000 |
| F | (−) TDS @ 2% (A × 0.02) | 16,000 |
| **G** | **(=) Net Payable (C − D − E − F)** | **6,40,000** |

Out of ₹8,00,000 worth of work, the contractor receives **₹6,40,000** in this bill.

Where does the rest go?
- ₹96,000 → GST (contractor collects, pays to government)
- ₹1,60,000 → Advance recovery (repaying the mobilization advance)
- ₹80,000 → Retention (held by client, released after DLP)
- ₹16,000 → TDS (client deposits to Income Tax, contractor claims in ITR)

## 6. TDS on RA Bills: Section 194C Explained

TDS (Tax Deducted at Source) is one of the most confusing aspects of RA Bills for contractors. Here's everything you need to know.

### Who Deducts TDS?

The **client** (owner, builder, or government department) deducts TDS before making payment to the contractor. The contractor receives the net amount after TDS deduction.

### TDS Rates Under Section 194C

| Contractor Type | TDS Rate |
|----------------|----------|
| Company or firm | 2% |
| Individual or HUF | 1% |
| If PAN not provided | 20% |

### Threshold Limits

TDS is applicable when:
- **Single payment** exceeds ₹30,000, OR
- **Cumulative payments** in a financial year exceed ₹1,00,000

If your contract value is above ₹1 lakh (which almost all construction contracts are), TDS applies from the first RA Bill itself.

### On What Amount is TDS Calculated?

As per **CBDT Circular No. 23/2017**, TDS should be deducted on the **gross amount excluding GST** — provided the GST component is shown separately in the invoice.

**Correct:** TDS = 2% of Gross Work Amount (before GST)

**Incorrect:** TDS = 2% of (Gross + GST) — this is wrong and many clients still do this.

If your client deducts TDS on the GST-inclusive amount, point them to this CBDT circular.

### How Contractors Claim TDS

1. Client deducts TDS and deposits it with the Income Tax Department
2. Client issues **Form 16A** (TDS certificate) quarterly
3. TDS appears in contractor's **Form 26AS** (tax credit statement)
4. Contractor claims this TDS as **tax already paid** while filing ITR
5. If TDS exceeds tax liability, contractor gets a **refund**

> **Pro tip:** Download your Form 26AS from the Income Tax portal every quarter. Cross-check that all TDS deducted by clients is reflected there. Missing TDS entries mean your client hasn't deposited the tax — and you won't get credit for it.

## 7. Retention Money: Everything You Need to Know

### What is Retention Money?

Retention money is a percentage of each RA Bill that the client holds back as security. It protects the client against:
- Defective work discovered after project completion
- Contractor abandoning the project
- Warranty claims during the Defect Liability Period (DLP)

### Typical Retention Rates

| Contract Type | Retention Rate | DLP Period |
|--------------|---------------|------------|
| Government (CPWD) | 10% | 12–24 months |
| Government (PWD) | 5–10% | 12 months |
| Private residential | 5–10% | 12 months |
| Commercial/IT parks | 5% | 12–24 months |
| Interior fit-outs | 5% | 6–12 months |

Most contracts cap retention at **5% of total contract value**. Once the cumulative retention reaches this cap, no further deduction is made.

### When is Retention Released?

Typically in two parts:

1. **50% on project completion** — when the client issues the completion certificate
2. **50% after DLP expiry** — after the defect liability period ends without major claims

### Common Disputes and Your Rights

Retention money is **your money** — the client holds it temporarily as security. Common issues:

- **Client delays release** beyond the DLP period — you can issue a legal notice
- **Client refuses release** citing minor defects — get specific defect list in writing, fix them, and demand release
- **Client has gone bankrupt** — retention money is an unsecured debt; you may need legal recourse
- **Interest on retention** — most contracts don't provide for interest, but some FIDIC contracts do

## 8. Common RA Bill Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

After working with 500+ contractors across Chandigarh, Mohali, Ludhiana, and Delhi NCR, here are the most common RA Bill mistakes we see:

### Mistake 1: Calculating TDS on the Wrong Base Amount

**Wrong:** TDS on (Gross + GST)
**Right:** TDS on Gross amount only (per CBDT Circular 23/2017)

Impact: Over-deduction of TDS by 12–18%, which means less cash in hand.

### Mistake 2: Applying GST Before Deducting Advance

**Wrong:** Calculate GST on net amount after advance deduction
**Right:** GST applies on the full gross amount. Advance recovery is a separate deduction.

### Mistake 3: Not Tracking Cumulative Quantities

Each RA Bill must show **total quantities to date**, not just the current period. If you lose track, you'll either under-bill (losing money) or over-bill (creating disputes).

### Mistake 4: Missing Retention Release Dates

Many contractors forget to follow up on retention release. After a 12-month DLP, you might have ₹5–10 lakhs stuck with the client. Set calendar reminders.

### Mistake 5: No Written Engineer Certification

Verbal approvals mean nothing when there's a payment dispute. Always get the client's engineer to **sign the measurement book** and the **RA Bill abstract**.

### Mistake 6: Submitting Bill on the Wrong Date

Most contracts specify a billing schedule — e.g., "RA Bill to be submitted by 5th of each month." Missing this deadline can push your payment by an entire month.

### Mistake 7: Using Wrong HSN Code for GST

Construction services typically use **HSN 9954**. Using the wrong code can trigger GST department notices. Double-check with your CA.

### Mistake 8: Not Reconciling Material Issues in the Same RA Bill

If your contract includes material reconciliation (issued vs consumed), submit it alongside the RA Bill. Delays in material reconciliation can hold up your payment.

## 10. Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between RA Bill and final bill?

An RA Bill is an **interim** payment based on work done so far. The **final bill** is raised after project completion and includes all remaining quantities, final measurements, price adjustments, and retention release. The final bill settles the full contract account — no more bills can be raised after it.

### Can GST be claimed on retention money?

Yes. GST is applicable on the **full gross amount** of the RA Bill, including the portion that is held as retention. The contractor pays GST on the full invoiced amount, even though they receive less cash due to retention. The client gets input tax credit on the full GST amount.

### What happens if the client doesn't pay the RA Bill on time?

Most contracts specify a payment timeline (e.g., 30 days from submission). If the client delays beyond this:
1. Send a written reminder citing the contract clause
2. If still unpaid after 60 days, issue a legal notice
3. You may be entitled to **interest on delayed payment** (check your contract)
4. In extreme cases, you can **suspend work** as per contract provisions

### How many RA Bills can be raised in a project?

There's no fixed limit. RA Bills are raised as per the billing schedule in your contract — typically **monthly**. A 12-month project will have 11 RA Bills plus 1 final bill. Some contracts allow milestone-based billing instead of monthly.

### Is TDS deducted on the GST amount in an RA Bill?

**No.** As per CBDT Circular No. 23/2017, TDS under Section 194C should be deducted on the **gross amount excluding GST**, provided GST is shown separately in the invoice. If your client deducts TDS on the GST-inclusive amount, share this circular with them to get the correct deduction.
