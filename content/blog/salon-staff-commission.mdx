## Quick Answer

Salon staff commission in India is typically calculated as a percentage of services performed — usually **20–40% on haircuts and colour**, **15–25% on facial and skin treatments**. Monthly commission = Sum of (each service value × commission %). Most salons pay a **base salary plus commission**, with the commission activating above a monthly service threshold (e.g., 20% on all services above ₹25,000/month).

## Common Commission Models in Indian Salons

There's no single "right" model — it depends on your salon's size, positioning, and the experience level of your staff. Here are the three models used across salons in Chandigarh, Mohali, Delhi NCR, and other Indian cities.

### Model 1: Pure Commission (No Base Salary)

Staff keeps **30–40%** of every service they perform. No fixed salary.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Zero fixed cost for owner | Staff may cherry-pick high-value clients |
| Attracts confident, senior stylists | Unpredictable income for staff (slow months hurt) |
| Simple to calculate | Hard to retain — staff leaves for any better offer |
| Motivates high performance | Staff may rush through services to do more volume |

**Best for:** Premium salons with established, senior freelance stylists who bring their own clientele.

**Not suitable for:** New salons building a client base, or salons with junior/trainee staff.

### Model 2: Base Salary + Commission (Most Common)

Fixed monthly salary + commission on services above a target threshold.

- **Base salary:** ₹8,000–15,000/month (varies by city and experience)
- **Commission:** 15–25% on all services performed above a monthly target
- **Target:** Usually ₹25,000–40,000/month depending on salon type

This is the most popular model in Indian salons because it gives staff income security while still motivating them to perform.

| Base Salary Range | City Tier | Experience Level |
|-------------------|-----------|-----------------|
| ₹8,000–10,000 | Tier 2–3 (Mohali, Ludhiana, Jaipur) | Junior (0–2 years) |
| ₹10,000–15,000 | Tier 2 (Chandigarh, Pune, Ahmedabad) | Mid-level (2–5 years) |
| ₹15,000–20,000 | Tier 1 (Delhi, Mumbai, Bangalore) | Mid-level (2–5 years) |
| ₹20,000–30,000 | Tier 1 metro | Senior (5+ years) |

### Model 3: Tiered Commission (Best for Motivation)

Commission rate increases as staff hits higher revenue targets. This creates strong motivation because higher tiers are **retroactive** — when you cross a tier, the higher rate applies to all services from the first rupee.

| Monthly Service Revenue | Commission Rate | Example Payout on ₹80,000 |
|------------------------|----------------|---------------------------|
| Up to ₹30,000 | 15% | — |
| ₹30,001–₹50,000 | 20% | — |
| Above ₹50,000 | 25% | ₹80,000 × 25% = **₹20,000** |

**Why retroactive works:** If Priya does ₹48,000 in services, she gets 20% on all ₹48,000 = ₹9,600. But if she pushes to ₹52,000, she jumps to 25% on all ₹52,000 = ₹13,000. That extra ₹4,000 of services earned her an extra ₹3,400 in commission. This creates a powerful push to cross each tier.

> **Pro tip:** Set your first tier threshold at 70% of what your average stylist does naturally. This means most staff will cross into the commission zone, keeping morale high. Only the underperformers miss out — and that's the signal you need.

## How to Calculate Monthly Commission: Step by Step

Let's walk through a real example.

### Staff Member: Priya

- **Base salary:** ₹10,000/month
- **Commission model:** 20% on services above ₹25,000 monthly target
- **Month:** March 2025

### Priya's Services This Month

| Date | Service | Client | Amount (₹) |
|------|---------|--------|------------|
| 3 Mar | Hair colour + blow dry | Anita | 2,800 |
| 5 Mar | Facial cleanup | Renu | 600 |
| 8 Mar | Bridal trial makeup | Neha | 3,500 |
| 10 Mar | Haircut + styling | Walk-in | 500 |
| 14 Mar | Hair colour highlights | Pooja | 4,200 |
| 18 Mar | Full body wax + facial | Simran | 2,400 |
| 22 Mar | Keratin treatment | Preeti | 6,000 |
| 25 Mar | Hair colour + cut + blow dry | Walk-in | 3,000 |
| 28 Mar | Party makeup | Kavita | 2,500 |
| 30 Mar | Haircut × 3 clients | Various | 1,500 |

### Calculation

| Step | Description | Amount (₹) |
|------|-------------|------------|
| 1 | Total services performed | 27,000 |
| 2 | Minus: Commission activation target | 25,000 |
| 3 | Commission base (Step 1 − Step 2) | 2,000 |
| 4 | Commission amount (Step 3 × 20%) | 400 |
| 5 | Base salary | 10,000 |
| **6** | **Total payout (Step 4 + Step 5)** | **10,400** |

Priya takes home **₹10,400** this month.

If Priya had done ₹45,000 in services:
- Commission base: ₹45,000 − ₹25,000 = ₹20,000
- Commission: ₹20,000 × 20% = ₹4,000
- Total payout: ₹10,000 + ₹4,000 = **₹14,000**

### Excel Formula for Commission Calculation

If cell B2 contains the total services amount and your target is ₹25,000 with 20% commission rate:

```
=IF(B2 > 25000, (B2 - 25000) * 0.20, 0)
```

For tiered commission (15% up to ₹30K, 20% up to ₹50K, 25% above ₹50K — retroactive):

```
=IF(B2 > 50000, B2 * 0.25, IF(B2 > 30000, B2 * 0.20, IF(B2 > 0, B2 * 0.15, 0)))
```

Add the base salary separately:

```
Total Payout = Base Salary + Commission
```

## Commission by Service Category: Typical Indian Salon Rates

Not all services earn the same commission. Higher-skill services and product-intensive treatments typically have different rates.

| Service Category | Typical Commission | Example Service | Service Price (₹) | Commission Earned (₹) |
|-----------------|-------------------|-----------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Haircut (ladies) | 25–30% | Cut + blow dry | 500 | 125–150 |
| Haircut (gents) | 30–40% | Gents cut | 200 | 60–80 |
| Hair colour | 15–20% | Full colour | 2,500 | 375–500 |
| Highlights/balayage | 15–20% | Partial highlights | 3,500 | 525–700 |
| Facial/cleanup | 20–25% | Fruit cleanup | 500 | 100–125 |
| Waxing | 20–30% | Full legs | 600 | 120–180 |
| Threading | 30–40% | Eyebrow + upper lip | 100 | 30–40 |
| Bridal package | 10–15% | Full bridal | 15,000 | 1,500–2,250 |
| Nail art | 25–35% | Gel nail art set | 800 | 200–280 |
| Hair spa/treatment | 20–25% | Keratin treatment | 5,000 | 1,000–1,250 |
| Makeup (party) | 15–20% | Party makeup | 2,500 | 375–500 |

### Why Bridal Commission Is Lower

Bridal packages involve high product cost (makeup products, accessories, draping material) and often require 3–4 hours of work. The margin on a ₹15,000 bridal package after product costs might only be ₹8,000–10,000. A 10–15% commission on the full package value is fair when you consider the product cost is absorbed by the salon.

### Why Threading/Basic Cuts Have Higher Commission

These are low-product, high-skill services. The salon's cost is almost zero (a thread costs ₹2, scissors have no per-use cost). The stylist's skill IS the product — so a higher commission percentage is justified, even though the absolute amount is small.

## Tracking Commission Without Disputes: Best Practices

Commission disputes are the **#1 reason** stylists leave salons. Here's how to prevent them.

### 1. Print a Monthly Commission Statement — Staff Signs It

At the end of every month, print a statement showing:
- Every service the staff member performed (date, service, client, amount)
- Total service revenue
- Commission calculation (target, rate, amount)
- Base salary
- Final payout

Have them **read and sign** it before you process the payment. This eliminates "you calculated wrong" disputes.

### 2. Track Every Service Against Staff Name

Don't just track total salon revenue — track who did what. Your appointment register or Google Sheet must have a **Stylist** column. No exceptions. Even for walk-ins — whoever does the service, their name goes on the record.

### 3. Share Running Totals Mid-Month

Around the 15th of each month, share each staff member's running total. This serves two purposes:
- **Motivation** — "You're at ₹22,000 — just ₹3,000 more to hit your commission target!"
- **No month-end surprises** — staff knows roughly what to expect in their payout

### 4. Separate Retail Product Commission

If your staff sells retail products (shampoo, serum, conditioner) to clients, track this separately. Retail commission is typically **10–15%** — lower than service commission because the salon buys the product at cost.

| Revenue Type | Commission Rate |
|-------------|----------------|
| Services performed | 15–30% (per category) |
| Retail product sales | 10–15% |
| Membership/package sales | 5–10% (one-time on sale) |

### 5. Handle Disputes Transparently

If a staff member questions their commission:
1. Pull up the service log — show every service attributed to them
2. Walk through the calculation together
3. If there's a genuine error, correct it immediately
4. If it's a disagreement about attribution (e.g., "I started the service, she finished it"), establish a clear rule: **whoever does 50%+ of the service gets the commission**

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is salon staff commission taxable in India?

Yes. Commission paid to salon staff is part of their **salary income** and is taxable under the Income Tax Act. If a staff member's total annual income (base salary + commission) exceeds **₹2,50,000** (old regime) or **₹3,00,000** (new regime), they are liable for income tax. As an employer, you should deduct TDS under Section 192 if their annual income exceeds the basic exemption limit. For most salon staff earning ₹10,000–20,000/month, the total annual income stays below the exemption limit — so TDS is usually not applicable.

### How to handle commission for trainee staff?

Trainees (0–6 months experience) typically don't earn commission — they're on a fixed stipend of ₹5,000–8,000/month while learning. After the training period, transition them to the base + commission model at the junior rate (15–20% commission above a low target like ₹15,000/month). This gives them a clear incentive to improve while keeping your costs manageable during the training phase.

### Should commission be paid on retail products sold?

Yes, but at a **lower rate** than services — typically 10–15%. Retail commission motivates staff to recommend products during services ("This serum would be great for your hair type — we have it for ₹650"). Without retail commission, staff has no incentive to sell products, and your retail revenue will be near zero. Keep retail commission separate from service commission in your tracking.

### What is a fair target for commission activation?

Set the target at **60–70% of what your average stylist generates naturally** in a month. If your average mid-level stylist does ₹35,000–40,000 in services, set the target at ₹25,000. This ensures:
- Most staff crosses the threshold (maintains morale)
- Underperformers are visible (they consistently miss the target)
- High performers earn significantly more (rewarding excellence)

Don't set targets so high that only your top performer crosses them — that demoralises everyone else. And don't set them so low that commission becomes automatic — that removes the incentive to push harder.
