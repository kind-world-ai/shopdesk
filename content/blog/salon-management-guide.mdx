## Quick Answer

Managing a salon in India requires tracking **6 key areas**: appointment scheduling, staff management and commissions, inventory control, billing and GST compliance, daily cash reconciliation, and monthly profitability review. The most common failure point for Indian salons is **poor cash flow tracking** — most owners don't know their actual monthly profit. This guide covers each area with practical steps, formulas, and benchmarks specific to the Indian salon industry.

## 1. Appointment Management: Preventing Revenue Leakage

Your appointment book is your revenue book. Every empty slot is lost revenue that never comes back. Yet most salons in Chandigarh, Mohali, and across India still use paper registers — or worse, rely on memory.

### Why Paper Registers Fail

- **Double bookings** — two clients show up for the same slot with the same stylist
- **No-show tracking** — you can't track patterns when there's no data
- **Walk-in blindness** — walk-ins account for 40–60% of revenue in Indian salons, but paper registers rarely capture them properly
- **No historical data** — you can't see which days are busiest, which services are most popular, or which time slots have the lowest utilization

### Tracking Appointments in Google Sheets

You don't need expensive salon software. A simple Google Sheet with these columns does the job:

| Column | Purpose | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Date | Service date | 05-Mar-2025 |
| Time Slot | Appointment time | 11:00 AM |
| Client Name | Customer name or walk-in | Priya Sharma |
| Phone | For follow-up | 98765-XXXXX |
| Service | What was done | Hair colour + blow dry |
| Stylist | Who performed it | Ritu |
| Amount (₹) | Bill value | 2,500 |
| Payment Mode | Cash / Card / UPI | UPI |
| Type | Booking or walk-in | Walk-in |
| Status | Done / No-show / Cancelled | Done |

### Measuring Appointment Utilization

This is the single most important metric for your salon's capacity:

**Utilization Rate = (Appointments Done ÷ Total Available Slots) × 100%**

Example: You have 3 chairs, each available for 8 hours with 30-minute average service time = 16 slots per chair × 3 chairs = **48 total slots per day**. If you serve 30 clients, your utilization is **62.5%**.

### Industry Benchmarks for Indian Salons

| Day Type | Poor | Average | Good | Excellent |
|----------|------|---------|------|-----------|
| Weekdays (Mon–Thu) | Below 40% | 40–60% | 60–75% | 75%+ |
| Fridays | Below 50% | 50–70% | 70–85% | 85%+ |
| Weekends (Sat–Sun) | Below 60% | 60–80% | 80–92% | 92%+ |
| Festival weeks | Below 70% | 70–85% | 85–95% | 95%+ |

If your weekday utilization is below 50%, you have a marketing problem. If your weekend utilization is below 70%, you have a booking management problem.

## 2. Staff Management: Commission, Attendance, Performance

Staff management is where most salon owners in India either thrive or drown. Get commissions wrong and your best stylists leave. Get them right and they become your biggest growth engine.

### Common Commission Models in Indian Salons

| Model | How It Works | Best For |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Fixed salary only** | ₹12,000–25,000/month, no commission | Juniors, trainees, shampoo assistants |
| **Fixed + commission** | ₹10,000 base + 15–25% of services done | Mid-level stylists — most common model |
| **Pure commission** | 30–50% of services done, no base | Senior freelance stylists, top performers |
| **Tips model** | Fixed salary + they keep all tips | Premium salons |

### Setting Fair Commission Rates: Example Calculation

Let's say stylist Ritu does ₹1,50,000 worth of services in a month:

| Component | Calculation | Amount (₹) |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| Base salary | Fixed | 12,000 |
| Service revenue | Total services done | 1,50,000 |
| Commission @ 20% | 1,50,000 × 20% | 30,000 |
| Product sales bonus @ 5% | Sold ₹20,000 in products × 5% | 1,000 |
| **Total CTC** | **Base + Commission + Bonus** | **43,000** |

**Rule of thumb:** Your total staff cost (all salaries + commissions) should be **30–40% of revenue**. If it's above 45%, you're overpaying. Below 25%, you'll lose good people.

### Tracking Individual Staff Performance

Track these monthly for every staff member:

- **Services per day** — how many clients they handle (target: 8–12 for mid-level)
- **Revenue per staff** — total billing generated (target varies by city: ₹80K–1.5L in Tier 1)
- **Average ticket size** — are they upselling? (higher is better)
- **Client requests** — how many clients specifically ask for them? (loyalty indicator)
- **No-shows** — are their clients reliable?

### Avoiding Staff Disputes

Commission disputes are the #1 reason stylists leave salons. Three rules:

1. **Everything in writing** — commission structure in the appointment letter, signed by both
2. **Calculate and share monthly** — show them the breakdown by the 3rd of every month
3. **Use a system, not memory** — whether it's a Google Sheet or ShopDesk, let the numbers speak

> **Pro tip:** Share a monthly performance card with each stylist showing their services count, revenue, commission earned, and ranking among peers. This creates healthy competition without drama.

## 3. Inventory Management: Stop the Bleeding

Ask any salon consultant and they'll tell you: **inventory is where most salon profits disappear**. Product wastage, over-use, theft, and expired stock silently eat your margins.

### The Three Inventory Killers

**1. Over-use per service:** Your stylist uses 30ml of hair colour when 20ml is enough. Over a month with 200 colour jobs, that's 2 litres wasted — roughly ₹3,000–5,000 in lost product.

**2. Pilfer and personal use:** Small products walk out the door. Shampoo sachets, cotton pads, nail polish — individually cheap, collectively expensive.

**3. Expired stock:** Buying 6 months of supply because of a discount, then watching 20% expire. The "discount" cost you money.

### Tracking Issued vs Consumed Per Service

For every high-value product, track:

| Product | Issued (Month) | Services Done | Expected Usage | Actual Usage | Variance |
|---------|----------------|---------------|----------------|--------------|----------|
| L'Oréal Hair Colour (tube) | 40 tubes | 180 colour services | 36 tubes (5 services/tube) | 40 tubes | **+4 over** |
| Wella Shampoo (litre) | 8 litres | 450 washes | 6 litres (75 washes/litre) | 8 litres | **+2 over** |
| Facial cream (pack) | 15 packs | 60 facials | 12 packs (5 facials/pack) | 15 packs | **+3 over** |

If your actual usage consistently exceeds expected usage by more than 10%, you have a wastage or theft problem. Investigate.

### Reorder Level Calculation

Never run out of stock on a Saturday. Use this formula:

**Reorder Level = (Daily Usage × Supplier Lead Time) + Safety Stock**

Example for hair colour:
- Daily usage: 2 tubes
- Supplier lead time: 5 days (order to delivery)
- Safety stock: 5 tubes (buffer for weekends and festivals)
- **Reorder Level = (2 × 5) + 5 = 15 tubes**

When stock hits 15 tubes, place an order. Simple — but most salons only reorder when they run out.

### Price Increase Strategy

Check your supplier prices every quarter. If raw material prices have gone up, your service prices should too. Don't wait for the increase to eat your margins for 6 months.

**Rule of thumb:** If your product costs go up by 10%, increase service prices by 5–7%. You absorb some, you pass some. Clients accept gradual increases; they resist sudden jumps.

## 4. GST Compliance for Salons: What You Actually Need to Know

GST is simpler for salons than most owners think. Here's what matters.

### GST Rate for Salon Services

**18% GST** on all beauty and wellness services — haircuts, styling, facials, manicure, pedicure, spa treatments, makeup, bridal packages. This is uniform across India.

**SAC Code:** 998271 (beauty and physical well-being services)

### Registration Threshold

- **Mandatory registration:** Annual turnover exceeds ₹20 lakh (₹10 lakh for special category states — NE states, Himachal, Uttarakhand, J&K)
- **Voluntary registration:** You can register even below the threshold — useful if you want to claim input tax credit on products and equipment

### Pricing Strategy: Inclusive vs Exclusive

You have two options:

**Exclusive pricing:** Service price ₹1,000 + GST 18% = Client pays ₹1,180
- Transparent — client sees the tax separately
- Better for B2B clients (corporate bookings) who need GST invoices

**Inclusive pricing:** Service price listed as ₹1,180 (inclusive of GST)
- Cleaner for walk-in clients — no sticker shock
- You extract GST: ₹1,180 ÷ 1.18 = ₹1,000 (base) + ₹180 (GST)

**Pick one and stick to it.** Switching confuses clients and staff. Most Indian salons in the ₹500–2,000 per service range use inclusive pricing.

### GST Invoice Requirements

Every GST invoice must include:

- Your GSTIN (15-digit number)
- Invoice number (sequential)
- Date of service
- Customer name (and GSTIN if B2B)
- SAC Code: 998271
- Taxable value (before GST)
- GST amount (CGST 9% + SGST 9% for intra-state)
- Total amount

### Filing Schedule

| Return | Due Date | What It Contains |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| GSTR-1 | 11th of next month (monthly) or 13th of month after quarter (QRMP) | Outward supplies (your sales invoices) |
| GSTR-3B | 20th of next month (monthly) | Summary return + tax payment |
| Annual Return | 31st December | Full year summary |

> **Pro tip for small salons:** If your turnover is below ₹5 crore, opt for the **QRMP scheme** — file GSTR-1 quarterly instead of monthly. Less paperwork, same compliance.

## 5. Daily Cash Management: The 10-Minute End-of-Day Routine

This is the habit that separates profitable salon owners from those who wonder where their money went. Do this every night before leaving — it takes 10 minutes.

### Daily Closing Checklist

**Step 1: Count cash in drawer.** Physical cash count. Write it down.

**Step 2: Total up card payments.** Check your POS/card machine end-of-day receipt. Note the total.

**Step 3: Total up UPI payments.** Open PhonePe/Google Pay/Paytm business account. Total today's received payments.

**Step 4: Calculate today's total revenue.**

**Total Revenue = Cash + Card + UPI**

**Step 5: Cross-check with appointment register.** Total all service amounts from today's appointments/walk-ins. This should match your payment total (within ₹100–200 tolerance for rounding).

**Step 6: Note any discrepancies.** If your register shows ₹15,000 in services but you only collected ₹13,500 — find out why. Common reasons: staff discount given without permission, client left without paying, wrong amount entered.

**Step 7: Log in your revenue tracker.** Enter today's total in your daily tracking sheet with breakdown by payment mode.

### Daily Tracking Format

| Date | Cash (₹) | Card (₹) | UPI (₹) | Total (₹) | Clients | Avg Bill (₹) |
|------|----------|----------|---------|-----------|---------|---------------|
| Mon 3 Mar | 4,200 | 3,800 | 6,500 | 14,500 | 18 | 806 |
| Tue 4 Mar | 3,100 | 2,900 | 5,200 | 11,200 | 14 | 800 |
| Wed 5 Mar | 5,600 | 4,200 | 7,800 | 17,600 | 22 | 800 |

This simple table, maintained daily, tells you more about your business than any fancy software. After 30 days, you'll know exactly what your salon earns — not what you think it earns.

## 6. Monthly Profitability Review

**Revenue − Expenses = Profit.** Obvious — but 80% of Indian salon owners cannot tell you their actual monthly profit. They know revenue (roughly). They don't track expenses systematically.

### Revenue Categories

| Category | What It Includes | Typical Split |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Services | Haircuts, colour, facial, spa, bridal | 80–90% of revenue |
| Product retail | Shampoo, conditioner, serum sold to clients | 8–15% of revenue |
| Membership/packages | Pre-paid service packages | 2–5% of revenue |

### Expense Categories

| Category | What It Includes | Typical % of Revenue |
|----------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Staff cost | Salaries + commissions + PF + bonus | 30–40% |
| Rent | Monthly rent + maintenance | 10–20% |
| Products/consumables | Hair colour, shampoo, disposables | 8–15% |
| Utilities | Electricity, water, AC, WiFi | 3–5% |
| Marketing | Instagram ads, Google, pamphlets | 2–5% |
| Misc | Repairs, laundry, tea/coffee, supplies | 2–4% |

### KPIs to Track Monthly

| KPI | Formula | Good Benchmark |
|-----|---------|---------------|
| **Revenue per chair** | Total revenue ÷ Number of chairs | ₹80,000–1,50,000/month |
| **Revenue per staff** | Total revenue ÷ Number of service staff | ₹60,000–1,20,000/month |
| **Average bill value** | Total revenue ÷ Total clients | ₹600–1,500 (varies by city and positioning) |
| **Return visit rate** | Repeat clients ÷ Total clients × 100% | 40–60% (above 60% is excellent) |
| **Net profit margin** | Net profit ÷ Revenue × 100% | 20–35% (before owner's salary) |

### Monthly Review Template

At the end of each month, fill this in:

| Metric | This Month | Last Month | Change |
|--------|-----------|------------|--------|
| Total Revenue | | | |
| Total Expenses | | | |
| Net Profit | | | |
| Total Clients | | | |
| Avg Bill Value | | | |
| Staff Cost % | | | |
| Revenue/Chair | | | |

If any metric moves more than 10% from last month, investigate why. Growth should be gradual and sustainable — sudden spikes or drops signal something that needs attention.

## 7. Common Salon Management Mistakes

After working with salon owners across Chandigarh, Mohali, Panchkula, Ludhiana, and Delhi NCR, here are the mistakes we see most often:

### Mistake 1: Not Tracking No-Shows and Cancellations

A no-show costs you the service revenue AND the opportunity cost of the empty slot. Track every no-show, identify repeat offenders, and implement a policy — many salons now ask for a ₹200–500 advance booking deposit for first-time clients.

### Mistake 2: Manual Commission Calculation

Calculating commissions by hand at month-end is begging for disputes. One number wrong and trust is broken. Use a system — even a basic Google Sheet — that both you and your staff can see.

### Mistake 3: Inventory Counted Only When Stock Runs Out

By the time you discover you're out of a popular hair colour on a Saturday afternoon, you've lost 3–4 colour appointments worth ₹6,000–10,000. Weekly stock checks prevent this.

### Mistake 4: No Separation Between Personal and Business Finances

"The salon earned ₹3 lakh this month" means nothing if you took ₹50,000 for personal expenses without recording it. Open a separate bank account for the salon. Pay yourself a fixed salary. Everything else stays in the business account.

### Mistake 5: Not Following Up for Repeat Visits

Your existing clients are 5× cheaper to retain than acquiring new ones. A simple WhatsApp message — "Hi Priya, it's been 6 weeks since your last hair colour. Would you like to book this weekend?" — can bring back 20–30% of lapsed clients.

> **The pattern:** Most of these mistakes come from the same root cause — lack of a system. You don't need expensive software. You need a consistent way to track appointments, revenue, staff, and inventory. A well-built Google Sheet handles all of this.

## 9. Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a good profit margin for a salon in India?

A well-managed salon in India typically achieves **20–35% net profit margin** on services (before owner's salary). Below 15% usually indicates either over-staffing, high rent, or poor inventory control. Product retail can add another 5–10% margin. Premium salons in metro cities (Delhi, Mumbai, Bangalore) often achieve 25–40% because of higher average bill values, while Tier 2–3 city salons typically operate at 15–25%.

### How many staff members does a salon need?

A general formula: **1 stylist per 2 chairs** for full-service salons, plus 1 assistant for every 2–3 stylists, plus 1 receptionist if you have more than 4 chairs. A 6-chair salon typically needs 3 stylists, 1–2 assistants, and 1 receptionist = **5–6 staff total**. Adjust based on your average service time and peak hours — if most services take 60–90 minutes, you need fewer staff than a quick-service salon.

### What is a good average bill value for a salon?

This varies significantly by city and positioning:

| City Tier | Budget Salon | Mid-Range | Premium |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|---------|
| Tier 1 (Delhi, Mumbai) | ₹300–600 | ₹800–1,500 | ₹2,000–5,000 |
| Tier 2 (Chandigarh, Jaipur) | ₹200–400 | ₹500–1,000 | ₹1,200–3,000 |
| Tier 3 (smaller cities) | ₹150–300 | ₹400–700 | ₹800–1,500 |

If your average bill value is below your city's mid-range, focus on upselling — add-on services like deep conditioning, hair spa, or product recommendations during the service.

### How do I handle GST for home service visits?

Home visits are treated the same as in-salon services for GST purposes — **18% GST applies**. However, you can add a home visit surcharge (₹200–500) on top of the regular service price. Include this surcharge in the taxable value. For GST invoice purposes, your salon's GSTIN is used regardless of where the service is performed — your place of business remains the salon.

### What software do successful Indian salons use?

Most Indian salons in the ₹10–50 lakh revenue range don't use dedicated salon software — it's too expensive (₹2,000–5,000/month) and complex for their needs. Instead, successful salon owners use:

- **Google Sheets** (with a system like ShopDesk) for appointments, billing, and staff tracking — ₹999 one-time
- **WhatsApp Business** for client communication and booking confirmations — free
- **PhonePe/Google Pay** for UPI payments — free
- **A simple POS machine** for card payments — available from banks at ₹500–2,000

The key isn't the tool — it's having a **consistent system** that you use every single day. The best software in the world doesn't help if nobody opens it.
